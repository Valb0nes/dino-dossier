Dreadnoughtus: Dino Dossier

Fossil information

After four expeditions the entire team had found about 145 bones and one singular tooth. These 145 bones included 112 from a bigger Dreadnoughtus and about 30 bones from a smaller second Dreadnoughtus. In all of those bones most of the vertebrae has been found which includes the tail, the neck, the scapula, multiple ribs, part of the jaw, a couple toes, and a claw. The entire skeleton is only about 45.3% completed and that includes the skull.

Size Range

Their weight could differ from about 22 tons to 59 tons. Those different weights come from when they are adolescent to full grown adults. Their weight was compared to that of a dozen African Elephants put together. Their height can be between 26m long to 2 stories tall between adolescent and adult. They were considered to be one of the largest and heaviest dinosaurs in their time.

Breeding Behaviors

When they would breed the females would huddle together in very large groups wherever they decided to lay their eggs. This would help prevent anything from trying to steal their eggs. The males when they battle for mating rights it is always a death match for whoever the loser is. They also have sacks on their necks that they use to attract a mate. These sacks were there so that the males looked more appealing for the females even though the male would have to battle between other males for the winnings.

Communication

Dreadnoughtus have these sacks on their necks that are bright orange and filled with air. They are predicted to use these sacks for communication and mating purposes. These sacks run all along their necks. Other than those sacks nothing else is known about how the Dreadnoughtus communicated with each other.

Time period

The Dreadnoughtus lived in the Champanian/ maastrichtian of the cretaceous. In this time period many of the other dinosaurs lived too. This period was when most of the dinosaurs we know today have lived. This time period included many different plants that the dinosaurs fed on that are now extinct today.
